⚡ Reaction Mechanisms
Step-by-step breakdown of each major mechanism.

Free Radical Substitution — Ethane + Cl₂ (UV)

I

Initiation Slow

UV light causes homolytic fission of the Cl–Cl bond, producing two chlorine radicals.

Cl—Cl → 2 Cl•
II

Propagation — Step A Chain

Chlorine radical abstracts a hydrogen from ethane, forming HCl and an ethyl radical.

CH₃CH₃ + Cl• → CH₃CH₂• + HCl
III

Propagation — Step B Chain

Ethyl radical attacks Cl₂, forming chloroethane and regenerating a Cl radical.

CH₃CH₂• + Cl₂ → CH₃CH₂Cl + Cl•
IV

Termination

Any two radicals combine to end the chain. Possible combinations: Cl• + Cl•, CH₃CH₂• + Cl•, CH₃CH₂• + CH₃CH₂•

Cl• + Cl• → Cl₂
CH₃CH₂• + Cl• → CH₃CH₂Cl

Electrophilic Addition — Ethene + Br₂

I

Polarisation & Attack Slow (RDS)

The π electrons of the C=C bond polarise the Br–Br molecule (δ+Br–Brδ−). The electron-rich C=C attacks the δ+ Br, forming a carbocation intermediate and releasing Br⁻.

CH₂=CH₂ + Br–Br → ⁺CH₂–CH₂Br + Br⁻

Carbon atoms change hybridisation from sp² → sp³

II

Nucleophilic Attack Fast

Br⁻ (nucleophile) attacks the carbocation from the back, completing anti-addition to give 1,2-dibromoethane.

⁺CH₂–CH₂Br + Br⁻ → BrCH₂–CH₂Br

Electrophilic Addition — Propene + HBr (Markovnikov)

I

Proton Transfer → Carbocation Slow (RDS)

H⁺ (from HBr) attacks the C=C. Two carbocation intermediates form: a 2° and a 1° carbocation. The 2° carbocation is more stable (Markovnikov's rule) and is the major pathway.

CH₃–CH=CH₂ + HBr → CH₃–CH⁺–CH₃ + Br⁻ (major)
II

Br⁻ Attacks Carbocation Fast

Br⁻ attacks the 2° carbocation, giving the major product 2-bromopropane.

CH₃–CH⁺–CH₃ + Br⁻ → CH₃–CHBr–CH₃ (2-bromopropane)

Electrophilic Substitution — Benzene + HNO₃/H₂SO₄ (Nitration)

I

Generation of the Electrophile NO₂⁺

Sulfuric acid (proton donor) reacts with nitric acid (proton acceptor) to generate the nitronium ion NO₂⁺.

HNO₃ + 2H₂SO₄ ⇌ NO₂⁺ + 2HSO₄⁻ + H₃O⁺
II

Electrophilic Attack — Benzonium Ion Slow

NO₂⁺ attacks the electron-rich benzene ring. One carbon becomes sp³ hybridised, forming the benzonium (arenium) ion intermediate.

C₆H₆ + NO₂⁺ → [C₆H₆·NO₂]⁺ (benzonium ion)
III

Loss of H⁺ — Restore Aromaticity Fast

HSO₄⁻ removes H⁺ from the sp³ carbon, restoring the π system. H₂SO₄ is regenerated as a catalyst.

[C₆H₆·NO₂]⁺ + HSO₄⁻ → C₆H₅NO₂ + H₂SO₄

SN2 Mechanism (CH₃CH₂Br + OH⁻)
1

Concerted Back-Side Attack

HO⁻ (nucleophile) attacks the δ+ carbon from the back (180° to the leaving group). The C–Br bond breaks simultaneously as the C–OH bond forms, via a trigonal bipyramidal transition state.

HO⁻ + δ+CH₃CH₂–Brδ− → [HO···CH₂CH₃···Br]‡ → HOCH₂CH₃ + Br⁻
SN1 Mechanism ((CH₃)₃CBr + OH⁻)
1

Ionisation — Carbocation Formation Slow (RDS)

The C–Br bond breaks heterolytically, generating a stable tertiary carbocation (planar, sp² hybridised) and Br⁻.

(CH₃)₃C–Br → (CH₃)₃C⁺ + Br⁻
2

Nucleophilic Attack from Both Faces Fast

OH⁻ attacks the planar carbocation from either face, giving equal amounts of both enantiomers — a racemic mixture.

(CH₃)₃C⁺ + OH⁻ → (CH₃)₃C–OH (both enantiomers)
🔬 Aromatic Chemistry
Benzene reactions and the route to phenylamine (aniline).
Benzene → Nitrobenzene → Phenylamine
1

Nitration of Benzene Electrophilic Substitution

Benzene reacts with a mixture of concentrated HNO₃ and H₂SO₄ at 50–55°C to form nitrobenzene.

C₆H₆ + HNO₃ → C₆H₅NO₂ + H₂O

Temperature must stay below 55°C to avoid di- or tri-nitration.

2

Reduction of Nitrobenzene → Phenylamine Reduction

Nitrobenzene is reduced to phenylamine (aniline) using tin (Sn) and concentrated HCl under reflux, followed by alkaline workup with NaOH.

C₆H₅NO₂ + 6[H] → C₆H₅NH₂ + 2H₂O
Reagents: Sn / conc. HCl (reflux), then NaOH (aq)
❌ Cannot go directly from benzene to phenylamine in a single step. Nitrobenzene is the essential intermediate.
Why Benzene Undergoes Substitution, NOT Addition

Benzene's six delocalised π electrons form an exceptionally stable aromatic system (extra stability ≈ 150 kJ mol⁻¹). Addition reactions would destroy aromaticity and lose this stability. Substitution allows an electrophile to replace one H while preserving the aromatic π system.

Bond length1.40 Å (between C–C and C=C)
HybridisationAll C atoms sp² (normally)
Catalyst roleH₂SO₄ is regenerated — acts as catalyst

Distinguishing Functional Group Isomers by Spectra

Propanoic acid vs Methyl ethanoate — IR Spectra ▾

Propanoic acid (C₃H₆O₂, Mᵣ = 74): Shows a broad O–H stretch at 2500–3300 cm⁻¹ (carboxylic acid), C=O stretch at ~1710 cm⁻¹, and C–O stretch.

Methyl ethanoate (CH₃CO₂CH₃, Mᵣ = 74): C=O stretch at ~1735 cm⁻¹ (ester, higher than acid), C–O stretches at 1250 cm⁻¹ and 1050 cm⁻¹. No broad O–H absorption.

Propene vs Cyclopropane — IR & NMR ▾

IR distinction: Propene shows a C=C stretching vibration at ~1640 cm⁻¹. Cyclopropane has no such peak.

¹H NMR — Propene (C₃H₆): 4 different proton environments (a at 5.00 ppm, b at 4.93 ppm, c at 5.84 ppm, d at 1.65 ppm) with splitting patterns.

¹H NMR — Cyclopropane (C₃H₆): Only ONE proton environment (all 6 H equivalent) at δ = 0.20 ppm. Single singlet.

Propanal vs Propanone — Mass Spectra & NMR ▾

Both: Mᵣ = 58, molecular formula C₃H₆O.

Propanal mass spectrum: Base ion peak at m/z = 29 [HCO]⁺ or [C₂H₅]⁺. Fragment at m/z = 57 [C₃H₅O]⁺.

Propanone mass spectrum: Base ion peak at m/z = 43 [CH₃CO]⁺. This is the characteristic acylium ion.

Propanal ¹H NMR: 3 environments — CH₃ triplet (1.12 ppm), CH₂ multiple (2.47 ppm), CHO singlet (9.80 ppm).

Propanone ¹H NMR: Only 1 environment — both CH₃ groups equivalent at 2.16 ppm, singlet, no splitting.

NMR Splitting (n+1 Rule)

A proton with n equivalent neighbouring H atoms splits into n+1 lines.

Singlet (s)0 neighbours
Doublet (d)1 neighbour
Triplet (t)2 neighbours · ratio 1:2:1
Quartet (q)3 neighbours · ratio 1:3:3:1
Example — Propanoic acid (CH₃CH₂COOH):
CH₃ at 1.16 ppm → triplet (split by 2 CH₂ neighbours)
CH₂ at 2.38 ppm → quartet (split by 3 CH₃ neighbours)
COOH at 11.7 ppm → singlet
🧪 Chemical Tests
How to distinguish between pairs of organic compounds.
Propanoic acid
vs
Methyl ethanoate
Test

Add sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃) solution to each compound separately.

Observations

Propanoic acid: Effervescence (CO₂ gas produced).

Methyl ethanoate: No reaction / no effervescence.

Equation

2CH₃CH₂COOH + Na₂CO₃ → 2CH₃CH₂COONa + H₂O + CO₂↑
Methyl ethanoate does not react with Na₂CO₃ (ester is not acidic enough)

1° Alcohol (e.g. ethanol)
vs
2° Alcohol (e.g. propan-2-ol)
vs
3° Alcohol (e.g. 2-methylpropan-2-ol)
Test

Add acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ (orange) to each alcohol and warm gently.

Observations

1° alcohol: Orange → green (oxidised to aldehyde, then acid).
2° alcohol: Orange → green (oxidised to ketone).
3° alcohol: Stays orange — no reaction.

Aldehyde
vs
Ketone
Test 1 — Tollens'

Add Tollens' reagent (ammoniacal silver nitrate) and warm in a water bath.

Observations

Aldehyde: Silver mirror on test tube walls.
Ketone: No silver mirror.

Test 2 — Fehling's

Add Fehling's solution (deep blue Cu²⁺) and heat.

Observations

Aldehyde: Blue → brick-red precipitate (Cu₂O).
Ketone: Remains blue.

Alkene
vs
Alkane
Test

Add bromine water (Br₂/H₂O) to each compound.

Observations

Alkene: Reddish-brown bromine water decolourises (electrophilic addition).
Alkane: No decolouration in the dark.
